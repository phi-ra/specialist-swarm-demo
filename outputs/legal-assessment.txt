# Will A. win? Panel assessment

**Bottom line: MODERATE-TO-HIGH likelihood that A. wins (acquittal upheld, appeal rejected) — roughly 60–70%.**

## 1. What is actually charged (a framing correction)

The offence is **not** Art. 292 StGB (two of my specialists drifted into it). "Missachten eines gerichtlichen Verbots" here is the *sui generis* Übertretung attached to an **allgemeines/audienzrichterliches Verbot** — historically the Zurich cantonal regime (ZPO/ZH 1976), today **Art. 258–260 ZPO**. Art. 260 ZPO punishes intentional violation *auf Antrag* (Antragsdelikt) with a Busse. Two structural limits flow from this and both favour A.:
- A court prohibition may forbid only **widerrechtliche Störungen** — conduct the owner could independently enjoin under his exclusion right (Art. 641 Abs. 2 ZGB). It **crystallises an existing right; it cannot create new duties** (classic doctrine on the richterliches Verbot).
- It is a property-**exclusion** instrument. It is not designed to secure a **monetary claim**.

## 2. Scope of the prohibition × the fee regime — the decisive axis

Two readings of the dual-message sign ("Parkieren verboten" + "Besucher gestattet, ≤3 h, gegen Gebühr"):

- **Prosecution (conditional-permission):** payment is a *condition precedent* to the permission. No payment → no permitted status → the car sits under the ban → punishable Übertretung. *"Wer nicht zahlt, parkiert unbefugt."*
- **Respondent (two-regime):** the ban targets **unauthorised parking as such** (non-visitors, unmarked spaces, >3 h). A visitor on a marked space within 3 h is **within the permitted category**; failure to pay is a **fee default** — a public-law *Benutzungsgebühr* (Kausalabgabe, Art. 5/164 BV) or a contractual *Parkvertrag* claim — enforced by **Betreibung / administrative means**, not Strafrecht.

The two-regime reading is doctrinally cleaner: it does not conflate the **right to use** (status) with the **debt arising from use** (fee), and it respects that non-payment of a fee is not, by itself, a *widerrechtliche Störung* of the owner's property right. Swiss law channels fee non-payment to debt collection, not criminal sanction, absent an explicit Ordnungsbussen/penal norm — and the OBG does not create one for a municipal car-park fee. **Both cantonal instances adopted this reading.**

## 3. Legality principle (Art. 1 StGB) — the multiplier for A.

Art. 1 StGB (+ Art. 7 EMRK) commands the **Bestimmtheitsgebot**, **strict construction**, and the **Analogieverbot** (no extension to the accused's detriment); genuine ambiguity in a penal norm is resolved **in favorem rei**. A sign that in one breath forbids parking and in the next permits it is at least ambiguous as to whether *unpaid-but-otherwise-authorised* parking is criminal. Using a 1980 **ex parte** order to reach fee-evasion pushes toward prohibited analogy. This principle bites **even where the Federal Court reviews freely** — the court cannot cure the ambiguity by expanding the penal reach.

## 4. Standard of review when the STATE appeals an acquittal

- Standing of the Oberstaatsanwaltschaft is unproblematic (Art. 81 BGG); it bears a **qualified pleading burden** (Art. 42 Abs. 2, 106 Abs. 2 BGG).
- **Facts / evidence:** bound unless **arbitrary** (Art. 97 Abs. 1 / 105 BGG); *in dubio pro reo* as a *Beweiswürdigungsregel* collapses into a **Willkür** standard against the appellant. A **double concordant acquittal** makes this bar very high.
- **The catch for A.:** the *decisive* issue — the legal **scope** of the prohibition and its interplay with Art. 258/260 ZPO and Art. 1 StGB — is a **question of law reviewed freely**, so Willkür-deference does **not** shield the core point. A.'s protection there comes from the legality principle, not from deference.
- Net effect: on the fact-laden sub-questions (what the sign said, Vorsatz, whether A. was a visitor on a marked space ≤3 h, the Strafantrag) A. is strongly protected by Willkür + double acquittal; on the pure legal question he must win on the merits — but the merits and the *in dubio* interpretive rule lean his way.

## 5. Strongest counter-argument, and why it only partly bites

The state's best shot is that the **conditional-permission reading is plain meaning, not analogy**: "gestattet gegen Gebühr" makes payment constitutive of the permission, so unpaid parking is simply the prohibited act — no Bestimmtheitsgebot problem, no fee/prohibition conflation. This is **respectable and non-frivolous** — and it is almost certainly why the case sits before a **5-judge panel** (Art. 20 Abs. 2 BGG signals a *question of principle*, i.e. the court means to set doctrine). It does **not** clearly prevail because it (a) fuses use-entitlement with payment-obligation against the grain of Swiss fee doctrine, (b) repurposes an exclusion instrument to collect money, and (c) still confronts a genuinely dual-message sign that Art. 1 StGB resolves for the accused. But it is strong enough to keep this out of "high/near-certain" territory.

## 6. Open questions a court must still resolve

1. Is "gegen Gebühr" a **constitutive condition** of the permission or a **severable debt**?
2. May an Art. 258/260 ZPO Verbot validly forbid "parking without paying," or only *widerrechtliche Störungen* — and can it be used to **enforce a fee** at all?
3. Does a **1980 cantonal-era ex parte** order still satisfy Art. 1 StGB / Art. 7 EMRK determinacy under the federal ZPO?
4. Is the fee a **public-law Benutzungsgebühr** or contractual, and what enforcement channel exists?
5. **Vorsatz** and valid **Strafantrag** by the City (Antragsdelikt).
6. Allocation of **review standards** across the federal-law vs cantonal-law/fact sub-questions.

## 7. Why "moderate-to-high," not "high"

Pushing A. up: two reasoned acquittals; the clean two-regime doctrine; Art. 1 StGB's structural bias; the systemic channelling of fee non-payment to civil/administrative collection; Willkür-protection on all factual elements. Holding him back from near-certainty: the decisive issue is a **freely reviewable question of law**, so deference does not do the heavy lifting there; the conditional-permission reading is genuinely arguable; and the **5-judge composition** shows the Federal Court itself regards the point as open and worth a leading decision — a court does not convene a Fünferbesetzung to rubber-stamp an obvious result. A published decision could equally cut the other way, though the legality-principle pull makes a *pro-accused* clarifying rule the more natural outcome.

**Estimate: A. is more likely than not to prevail and see the acquittal confirmed — call it ~60–70%.** The single biggest swing factor is whether the panel treats "gegen Gebühr" as constitutive of the permission (state wins) or as a severable fee debt (A. wins); everything else in the doctrinal and procedural architecture tilts toward A.
